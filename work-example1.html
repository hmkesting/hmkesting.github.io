Trend analysis showing changes in ecosystem water use due to seasonal precipitation amount








Evapotranspiration (ET) is the flux of water from the Earth's surface to the atmosphere. It comprises evaporation as well as transpiration, which is the process of water moving through a plant evaporating from leaves and stems. ET affects water availability and can be an indicator of vegetation health. Using isotopes of hydrogen and oxygen, we conducted end-member splitting analysis (Kirchner and Allen, 2020) to determine how watershed inputs of summer and winter precipitation are split into outputs of runoff and ET. Figure 1 shows an example of a figure describing long-term average partitioning in a watershed (data are simulated since manuscript is in preparation for publication). We're specifically interested in how the fraction of ET sourced from summer precipitation varies with summer precipitation amount. However, this method relies on several assumptions that make it unsuitable for analyzing data at the annual timescale.










The end-member splitting method assumes that all inputs and outputs are accounted for. This becomes problematic at annual timescales because we don't know how much precipitation from previous years is supplying runoff. One way of addressing this is to examine the results under a variety of contradicting assumption and look for consistent trends as shown in figure 2. However, trends are rarely consistent and thus are difficult to interpret. Furthermore, this approach ignores another key assumption: that there is no change in storage during the sampling period for each data point which, in this case, is one year.





























Another option is to group years by the amount of summer precipitation and analyze differences between the groups. By grouping multiple years, we can assume that changes in storage are negligible compared to total flux amounts. We calculate the weighted average isotope values for each group before calculating the partitioning fractions, so we aren't assuming that all inputs or outputs are confined to a single year. However, this method doesn't show how partitioning changes over a range of values and we were unable to detect many statistically significant differences between the two groups.


















To solve this problem, we developed a bootstrapping numerical solution approach. We replaced every variable in the end-member splitting equations with the linear regression of that variable against the x-axis ( summer precipitation). We conducted 3000 bootstrapping iterations by repeating this process with random samples of the data, then used the percentiles from those iterations to create figure 4. This figure is preferable compared to others because it show a trend across the range of possible summer precipitation amounts, it clearly quantifies uncertainty, and it conforms to the assumptions of the end-member splitting method.
